# Refining Margins and Crack Spreads

---

## What is a Crack Spread?

A crack spread is the difference between the price of crude oil and the petroleum products refined from it. It represents the gross refining margin.

The term "crack" comes from the catalytic cracking process that breaks down (cracks) crude oil molecules into lighter products.

---

## Common Crack Spread Calculations

**Simple Gasoil Crack:**
Gasoil Price ($/bbl) - Brent Price ($/bbl) = Gasoil Crack

**3-2-1 Crack Spread (US benchmark):**
(2 × Gasoline + 1 × Heating Oil) / 3 - Crude Oil Price

**2-1-1 Crack (European):**
(1 × Gasoline + 1 × Gasoil) / 2 - Brent Price

Typical ranges:
- Normal: $10-20/bbl
- Strong: $20-35/bbl
- Crisis (2022): $50-60/bbl
- Weak: below $10/bbl (refinery shutdowns likely)

---

## Gasoil vs Gasoline Margins

Gasoil (diesel/heating oil) and gasoline margins often move inversely based on season:
- **Winter (Q4-Q1):** Gasoil premium — heating demand drives diesel/gasoil margins up
- **Summer (Q2-Q3):** Gasoline premium — driving season boosts gasoline margins
- **Shoulder months (March, October):** Transitions, refinery maintenance

The Brent-Gasoil spread specifically tracks European refining economics. London ICE Gasoil (LGO) is the benchmark for European middle distillate pricing.

---

## Refinery Complexity

Not all refineries are equal. Complexity determines which crude grades a refinery can process:

- **Simple (hydroskimming):** Can only run light, sweet crude. Low conversion.
- **Complex (cracking):** Can process medium/sour crude. Higher margins.
- **Very complex (coking):** Can process heavy, sour crude (like Canadian heavy). Highest potential margins.

Nelson Complexity Index (NCI):
- Simple: 2-5
- Medium: 6-9
- Complex: 10-14 (US Gulf Coast average ~12)

When heavy-light crude differentials widen, complex refineries benefit disproportionately.

---

## Seasonal Margin Patterns

**Q1 (Jan-Mar):** Moderate. Post-winter, heating demand fading. Refinery maintenance begins.
**Q2 (Apr-Jun):** Rising. Pre-driving season gasoline build. Refineries ramp up after turnarounds.
**Q3 (Jul-Sep):** Peak gasoline margins. Hurricane risk (US Gulf = 50% of US refining). Gasoil margins begin rising late Q3.
**Q4 (Oct-Dec):** Peak gasoil margins. Winter heating demand. Year-end tax-loss trading.

---

## Key Margin Indicators

- **Refinery utilization rate:** >93% = very tight margins, >96% = near max capacity
- **Product inventories vs 5-year range:** Below range = bullish margins
- **Crude-product time spread:** Forward curve structure signals future margin expectations
- **Freight rates:** Higher tanker costs compress margins for import-dependent regions

---

## Trading Implications

- Rising crack spreads → bullish for refined products, may eventually incentivize more refining → more crude demand
- Falling crack spreads → bearish for products, may trigger refinery run cuts → less crude demand
- Persistent high cracks → signal structural supply shortage in refined products
- Crack spread collapse → recession signal (demand destruction exceeds supply adjustment)
