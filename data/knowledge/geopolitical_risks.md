# Geopolitical Risk Factors for Oil Markets

---

## Strait of Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical oil chokepoint. Approximately 20-21 million barrels per day (about 20% of global supply) transit through this narrow waterway between Iran and Oman.

Key facts:
- Width at narrowest point: 33 km (21 miles)
- Shipping lanes: two 3 km wide channels (inbound/outbound) separated by a 3 km buffer
- Primary exporters: Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar (LNG)
- Closure scenario: immediate $20-40/bbl spike, sustained premium for duration
- Iran has repeatedly threatened closure during sanctions escalations
- US Fifth Fleet based in Bahrain provides deterrence

Historical incidents: 1988 tanker war (Iran-Iraq), 2019 tanker seizures, Houthi attacks on commercial vessels (2023-2024).

## Russia-Ukraine Conflict Impact

Russia was the world's #2 crude exporter before 2022 sanctions. The conflict fundamentally restructured global oil trade flows.

Key impacts:
- EU embargo on Russian seaborne crude (Dec 2022) and products (Feb 2023)
- G7 price cap: $60/bbl for crude, $45/$100 for products
- Russian crude redirected to India, China, Turkey at discounted prices (Urals discount $10-30 vs Brent)
- European refiners switched to Middle East, West African, US crude — higher cost
- Diesel/gasoil: Europe lost its primary supplier, prices spiked 40%+ in 2022
- Shadow fleet: 600+ aging tankers carrying Russian oil outside Western insurance

Trading implications: Urals-Brent spread is a sanctions compliance indicator. Narrowing = weaker enforcement. Widening = tighter enforcement or new sanctions.

## Iran Sanctions

Iran holds the world's 4th-largest oil reserves. Sanctions cycles directly impact 1-2 million bpd of supply.

Timeline pattern:
- Sanctions imposed → 1-2 mbpd removed from market → bullish
- Sanctions waived/lifted (2015 JCPOA) → 1-2 mbpd returned → bearish
- "Maximum pressure" (2018 Trump) → exports fell from 2.5 to 0.3 mbpd
- Enforcement varies: 2023-2024 saw 1.5+ mbpd exports despite sanctions (weak enforcement)

Key signal: US State Department statements on enforcement. Increased waivers = bearish. Tighter enforcement = bullish.

## Libya Disruptions

Libya's oil production is extremely fragile due to political instability (dual governments, militia control of facilities).

- Capacity: ~1.2 mbpd, actual production swings 0.1-1.2 mbpd
- Major fields: Sharara (300k bpd), El Feel (70k bpd), Es Sider terminal
- Disruptions are frequent (5-10 per year) and usually short (days to weeks)
- Market has partially priced in "Libya risk premium" — but major shutdowns still move prices $2-5

## Venezuela

Massive reserves (300+ billion barrels) but production collapsed from 2.5 mbpd (2015) to ~0.7 mbpd (2024).

- US sanctions since 2019 restrict exports and investment
- PDVSA infrastructure severely degraded — recovery takes years even if sanctions lifted
- Chevron license (2022) allows limited exports — ~200k bpd
- Full sanctions relief = 300-500k bpd additional within 12-18 months (not immediate)

## Red Sea / Houthi Attacks

Since late 2023, Houthi militants in Yemen have attacked commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Bab el-Mandeb strait.

- ~8 mbpd of oil and products normally transit this route
- Attacks forced rerouting via Cape of Good Hope (+10-14 days, +$1M per voyage)
- Impact on gasoil: European gasoil imports from Middle East/India face higher freight costs
- Freight rates for dirty tankers spiked 200-300%
- Insurance "war risk" premiums surged from 0.07% to 0.5-1.0% of cargo value

Trading signal: escalation of attacks = bullish for refined products, especially gasoil. De-escalation = bearish for freight, modestly bearish for products.

## Other Chokepoints

- **Suez Canal**: 5-6 mbpd, connects Mediterranean to Red Sea
- **Bab el-Mandeb**: 6-7 mbpd, southern entrance to Red Sea
- **Turkish Straits (Bosphorus)**: 3 mbpd, Russian/Kazakh crude to Med
- **Malacca Strait**: 16 mbpd, critical for Asian imports
- **Panama Canal**: 1 mbpd, US Gulf to Asia route (drought disruptions 2023-2024)
