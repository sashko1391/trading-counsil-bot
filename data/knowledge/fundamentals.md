# Oil Market Fundamentals

Reference guide for interpreting oil market structure, spreads, and forward curves.

---

## Contango vs Backwardation

### Contango
- Definition: futures price > spot price (upward sloping forward curve)
- Indicates oversupply or weak near-term demand
- Storage economics become profitable (buy spot, sell futures, store)
- Typical during inventory builds and demand destruction
- Deep contango signals: recession risk, OPEC+ non-compliance, demand collapse
- Example: April 2020 — WTI contango exceeded $10/bbl as storage filled up

### Backwardation
- Definition: spot price > futures price (downward sloping forward curve)
- Indicates tight near-term supply or strong prompt demand
- Discourages storage (no carry profit)
- Typical during supply disruptions and strong demand
- Deep backwardation signals: supply crisis, geopolitical risk premium, OPEC+ overcompliance
- Example: 2022 post-Ukraine invasion — Brent backwardation exceeded $5/bbl

### Trading implications
- Contango favours patient longs (roll yield negative for longs, positive for shorts)
- Backwardation favours being long (positive roll yield for longs)
- Structure shifts from contango to backwardation are bullish signals
- Structure shifts from backwardation to contango are bearish signals
- Monitor M1-M6 spread as a key structural indicator

## Crack Spreads

### Brent-Gasoil Crack Spread
- Measures refinery margin: Gasoil price minus Brent crude price
- Key profitability indicator for European refiners
- Typical range: $10-25/bbl
- Widening crack spread: strong distillate demand, refinery outages, or tight product supply
- Narrowing crack spread: weak demand, high refinery runs, product inventory builds

### 3-2-1 Crack Spread
- Approximates refinery margin: (2 x Gasoline + 1 x Heating Oil) / 3 - Crude price
- US-focused benchmark
- Seasonal pattern: peaks in summer (driving season gasoline demand)

### Crack Spread Signals
- Abnormally high cracks (>$30): potential demand destruction, margin reversion expected
- Abnormally low cracks (<$8): refinery run cuts likely, bullish for product prices
- Diverging Gasoil vs Gasoline cracks: shifts in product demand mix
- Pre-EIA report: crack spread moves can foreshadow inventory surprises

## Brent-WTI Spread

### What it measures
- Price differential between ICE Brent (global benchmark) and NYMEX WTI (US benchmark)
- Reflects relative Atlantic Basin supply/demand balance
- Typical range: $2-8/bbl (Brent premium)

### Key drivers
- US production growth narrows the spread (more domestic supply)
- Mideast/African supply disruptions widen the spread (Brent premium up)
- US export capacity constraints widen the spread (WTI discount)
- Permian pipeline capacity additions narrow the spread
- Seasonal US refinery maintenance can temporarily widen the spread

### Trading signals
- Spread above $8: potential US export arbitrage opportunity
- Spread below $2: unusual, suggests global oversupply or US-specific tightness
- Rapid widening: geopolitical risk premium on Brent, bullish for global prices
- Rapid narrowing: US-specific supply pressure, or global supply easing

## Forward Curve Structure

### How to read the forward curve
- Plot futures prices from front-month (M1) to deferred months (M2...M12+)
- Flat curve: balanced market, no strong directional bias
- Steep contango: oversupply, high inventory, bearish near-term
- Steep backwardation: supply deficit, low inventory, bullish near-term

### Key spreads to monitor
- M1-M2 (prompt spread): most liquid, reflects immediate supply/demand balance
- M1-M6 (6-month spread): medium-term structural view
- M1-M12 (annual spread): long-term market structure
- Calendar spreads: Dec-Dec spreads reflect annual supply/demand expectations

### Time spreads and inventory correlation
- Time spreads correlate strongly with inventory levels
- OECD inventory above 5-year average -> contango
- OECD inventory below 5-year average -> backwardation
- EIA weekly inventory changes can shift prompt spreads within hours

### Curve dynamics around events
- OPEC+ meetings: curve typically flattens pre-meeting on uncertainty
- EIA reports: prompt spread reacts to inventory surprise within minutes
- Geopolitical events: prompt premium spikes (backwardation deepens)
- Seasonal demand shifts: winter heating demand steepens front-end backwardation

## Supply and Demand Fundamentals

### Global supply structure
- OPEC+ controls ~40% of global production (~40 mbd of ~100 mbd)
- US shale: ~13 mbd, flexible but capital-disciplined post-2020
- Non-OPEC+ growth: US, Brazil, Guyana, Canada (key marginal supply)
- Spare capacity concentrated in Saudi Arabia, UAE (~3-5 mbd total)

### Global demand drivers
- Total demand: ~102-103 mbd (2024 levels)
- China: ~16 mbd, largest incremental demand source
- India: ~5.5 mbd, fastest growing major market
- US: ~20 mbd, largest total consumer
- Aviation fuel recovery post-COVID still incremental demand source
- Petrochemical demand (naphtha, LPG) growing in Asia

### Inventory indicators
- OECD commercial inventories: key benchmark (IEA monthly report)
- US EIA weekly data: most timely inventory signal
- Floating storage (tankers): visible via satellite/AIS data
- Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR): US at historically low levels post-2022 release
- Days of forward demand cover: best normalised inventory metric

### Key thresholds
- Global spare capacity below 2 mbd: tight market, price spikes likely
- OECD inventories >5-year avg by 100+ mb: bearish overhang
- US crude stocks below 400 mb: tight, bullish for WTI
- US crude stocks above 480 mb: loose, bearish for WTI

## Price Levels and Ranges (Historical Reference)

### Brent crude
- Long-term average (2015-2024): ~$65/bbl
- Post-COVID range (2021-2024): $50-130/bbl
- OPEC+ comfort zone: $75-90/bbl (balances budgets vs demand destruction)
- US shale breakeven: $45-55/bbl (varies by basin)
- Demand destruction zone: above $100/bbl (dependent on macro conditions)

### ICE Gasoil
- Typical range: $600-1000/mt
- Crack spread drives: $100-300/mt above Brent-equivalent
- Extreme highs (>$1200/mt): 2022 post-Ukraine diesel crisis
- Seasonal peak: Q4-Q1 (heating demand)

### Key psychological levels
- Brent $80: key pivot level, OPEC+ comfortable
- Brent $70: support, potential OPEC+ intervention
- Brent $90: resistance, demand concerns emerge
- Brent $100: major psychological level, political sensitivity
